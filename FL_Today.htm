Runners create colorful presence

Hash House Harriers find fun outings

By CORBY SCHUBERT
Times Writer

     On a Saturday afternoon in Cocoa Beach, the sand is packed with bikini-dad women and muscular surfers.

     It's a routine scene. But, most heads instantly pop up to stare at the sight of running men wearing red dresses.

     For a moment, it's difficult to tell which is funnier - the gender-bending runners or the people who seem perplexed by the sight.

     Who are these guys, anyway?
    
     They call themselves the Brevard Hash House Harriers. They parade along a trail in the sand, laughing like giddy school girls and waving at onlookers who rise up on their beach towels and shake their heads in amusement.

     Perhaps, the sun worshippers wonder why some of the men have painted their fingernails and shaved their legs. Explanations that they are simply celebrating the club's second annual "Red Dress Hash" only raise more questions.

     Believe it or not, these guys aren't alone. They are part of an organization of more than 1,200 chapters worldwide that adhere to the motto "Drinkers with a running problem."

     Though fitness experts might discount their choice of sport and warn kids not to try this at home, group members say their main goal is to have fun

     "It's not about winning; it's more about lampoonery and lunacy," said Tom Fleming, who is referred to by club members only by his moniker, "Edgar Allen Ho."

     In fact, after five runs, members are given a nickname by the group to provide both a sense of anonymity and freedom to those who wish to keep their business or personal lives separate from the club, Fleming said.

     The group hosts biweekly meets (called "hashes") at locations throughout the Space Coast, which includes a running event, followed by socializing, singing and beer-drinking games. Often, members don't wait until the end of the run to take part in the liquid fun.

     'You're not going to like this group if you're easily offended," said Kim Todd, who was nicknamed "Fireindahole" when she became a Harrier in 1993.

     Competitive running is strongly discouraged, as the Harriers' events are not races, but more of a game in which one or two hares (really just people) set out running, leaving behind a trail of flour. Fifteen minutes later, the pack of hounds (more people) set out, tracking the marks in hopes of catching up to, and snagging, the hares. The hares will, of course, leave their marks clearly, but cleverly, so as to outwit the pack. This is done by leaving false trails and by arranging for beer stops along the way.

     "When I invite people to come out, the first thing that scares them is the word 'run,'" Todd said. "But it's not about running - they can run, walk or crawl to get to the end. The point is to just 'enjoy the camaraderie with a good ,bunch of people."

     The Hash House Harriers, which was started in 1938 by a Britisher who gathered several expatriates for a "paper chase" in 'what is now Malaysia, has evolved .into a worldwide organization and has done so almost entirely by word of mouth, said Paul Nunges, Who co-founded the Brevard chapter with his wife and, at age 62, is its oldest member.   "I've been hashing since the early 1980s and I'll be doing this ,for as long as I can," he said.

     Nunges, who goes by the nickname "Kojak," also is a member of a subcommittee involved in organizing a global "inter-hash" in which about 4,000 Harriers from throughout the world will participate next year in India.

     "Globally, my experience is that there are a lot of military personnel (who are Harriers)," said Nunges. "I've known of runs at military bases in which generals participated. It's the same here - you might have doctors, lawyers and government employees in the group, (as well as) a guy who mows grass.

     Members say they enjoy the companionship of the group and doing something in direct contrast to their daily lives.

     "You leave the work and the everyday life and just go out and totally clear your head," said member Mark Blackledge, known in the club as "Potty Flavor."

    "We expect people to be shocked. We were having a blast walking down the sidewalk coming in here," said Shawn Todd, also known as "Yanksit," as he adjusted his dress.

     Before this year's Red Dress Hash officially began, the Harriers lined up for an impromptu gallivant across the stage at Coconuts, and a few members lingered to dance and show off their innovative outfits.

     While posing for group photos, the guys didn't show a hint of embarrassment as they flaunted their stuffed bras and high heels. One fellow Harrier winked and hiked his skirt to reveal a garter, which held his credit cards in place against his thigh.

      "It's better to run in a dress, because there's nothing restricting your legs," said Blackledge, who mingled with the group of about 45 Harriers, some of whom came from as far as Jacksonville, Kissimmee and Daytona Beach for the run.

     As the Harriers formed a circle to sing a few rather bawdy songs, one member set out running, leaving behind the trail of flour.

     Fifteen minutes later, the hounds set out in good spirits, tracking the marks in hopes of catching up to the hare, who had left marks to try to outwit and slow down the pack by leaving phony trails and arranging for beer stops along the way.

    The distance of the trail varies depending on the hare's personal preference, with most hashes covering a 3- to 6- mile distance. The Red Dress Hash, however, covered only about a mile and a half.

    "Typically, our runs are held in parks or in the woods," Fleming said. "This was a pub crawl (type of) run, which we have about twice each year, so it was a little shorter."

     The trail first led the hounds along the side of State Road A1A and onto the sand in Cocoa Beach, with a rest stop at the halfway point at the Beach Shack bar for foamy refreshments.

     By that time, most members had worked themselves into a sweaty lather and were eager to cool off with a few cold beers. When the "run" resumed shortly thereafter, it remained at the same pace it had begun: a brisk walk.

     The second half of the hash took the giddy Harriers along the sidewalks of several streets in Cocoa Beach and in and out of several taverns. It concluded at a small beach bar, where members formed a circle around a large block of ice and prepared for the traditional "accusations" and "infractions."

     The Harriers lightheartedly accused one another of hashing unfairly, such as taking a shortcut which leads off the trail, and pointed out infractions which members made along the way.

      "It's basically an excuse to drink. When they've been accused, they sit on the ice with a full Beer," Fleming said. "We sing a song and they drink it down. Anything they don't finish (is poured) on their head."

     Having the nerve (if not the proper body shape) to ran in a red dress once a year is not the only requirement to be a Harrier; members must be at least 21 years of age. Instead of collecting membership dues, each Harrier contributes $5 per ran to cover the cost of beer and other refreshments.

     "It's not just about coming out in red dresses," said Kim Todd, who has participated in themed runs in Brevard in which the Harriers wore Halloween costumes, wedding dresses and togas.

     "I know I can call on any of these people if I need help for anything,- she said. "Gender doesn't have anything to do with it - it's more the attitude of the person. All the people are here to have a good time. They're looking forward to the trail and where it ends."
